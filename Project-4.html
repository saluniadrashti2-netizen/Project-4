The Architecture of Doubt Conspiracy

How are conspiracy theories constructed, and how do they challenge modern society?

Thesis

Conspiracy theories are constructed by exploiting fear and bias and are spread through social media which allows misinformation to influence society.

02 — Personal Connection

Why This Topic Matters

As more and more users are being exposed to conspiracy theories on social media and other forms of mass media, I have been motivated to further investigate how this type of exposure and engagement can shape people's beliefs. This has led me to raise concerns about the increasing amount of conspiracy content being created via social media, a lot of which presents untrue or misleading information to users on multiple platforms. This in return creates extreme disparities in our society, due to the disengagement between some users and others that oppose their beliefs.

This challenge makes me wonder how these types of conspiracy theories originated and why they are believed with such fervor by many people online. What fascinates me amidst all of this is the idea of knowing that various realities exist beneath the surface — and this fascination has evolved into a desire to uncover those realities as I become more aware of the fact that the world is not what it appears to be, and that most things are not as simple or perfect as they let us think, particularly with social and political issues. I want to explore the truth and compare it against what is exaggerated and against falsehoods.

What I find the most interesting about this subject is the way in which information can sway and influence anyone, including myself. The fact that we are unable to discern what is actually true and what is actually false is something that I'm unable to fathom. This topic becomes a matter of social concern because misinformation on social media can shape civic attitudes and undermine evidence-based decision making — and how misinformation can affect the way in which we function as a democracy.

03 — Background

Understanding the Topic

Political conspiracy theories have been around for quite some time, but the manner in which they have spread has evolved. In the past, people were influenced by what they listened to on the radio, what they read in the newspaper, and what they watched on television. Political elites influenced the general public through these mediums. However, today social media has revolutionized the manner in which these theories are spread. Political influencers can now easily reach millions of young people on these platforms, making them get influenced easily. As seen in Penn State's research, social media environments reward emotionally charged content, which makes conspiracy narratives especially likely to spread (Čejková 2). This has led to politics becoming a part of the internet culture. Research has also shown that education and political knowledge do not necessarily shield people from being influenced by conspiracy theories, but rather, are based on psychological factors. New research suggests that events happening worldwide are nurturing underlying emotions that make people more willing to believe in conspiracies. Experiments have revealed that feelings of anxiety make people think more conspiratorially (Moyer 4). More importantly, higher education is also not an indication of someone's lack of faith in conspiracies. Miller, Saunders, and Farhart found that politically knowledgeable conservatives were more likely to endorse conspiracy theories that aligned with their worldview, especially when they had low trust in others (Miller, Saunders, and Farhart 7). This has implications in the modern world because young people today are growing up in a world in which social media is very prevalent and significant. Therefore, understanding how this came to be would provide insights on how misinformation spreads easily in modern times.

04 — Main Arguments

Three Central Claims

01 Claim 1

Institutional Distrust Fuels Conspiracy Beliefs

Conspiracy theories develop because people already distrust powerful institutions like governments and corporations. When people feel that those in power are hiding the truth, they become willing to hear from various alternative narratives, even from those that lack credible evidence. This pre-existing skepticism creates ground for misinformation to take root and spread.

Evidence

People who already have conspiratorial thinking patterns are significantly more likely to believe in media bias conspiracies. (Jolley et al. 466)

This shows that conspiracy theories build on existing distrust rather than just new information. When people already believe institutions are dishonest, they are more likely to accept misleading claims.

Evidence

Partisan attachment has long been known to drive opinions… and is tied to resistance or acceptance of political narratives. (Uscinski et al. 471)

02 Claim 2

Mixing Truth with Misinformation Makes Conspiracies Believable

Conspiracy theories are constructed by combining real facts with misleading or unrelated information to appear authentic. This technique makes it difficult for ordinary people to distinguish fact from fiction.

Evidence

The ID2020 conspiracy used real events like the Event 201 pandemic simulation and Microsoft patents to suggest a secret global plan. (Thomas 3)

Because real events are included, the conspiracy appears legitimate, even though the connections made are misleading.

Evidence

"Conspiracy beliefs depend not only on exposure to information suggesting a conspiracy but also on predispositions" (Uscinski et al., 2016).

03 Claim 3

Social Media Accelerates the Spread of Conspiracy Theories

Social media platforms help conspiracy theories spread rapidly to large audiences. Recommendation algorithms prioritize content that generates strong emotional reactions, and conspiracy theories based content, by default, install fear, anger, and outrage. This means misinformation often travels faster than accurate information.

Evidence 1

A small number of highly active accounts were able to influence algorithms and spread ID2020 conspiracies widely. (Thomas 13)

Algorithms prioritize engaging content, so sensational conspiracy theories spread faster than accurate information.

Evidence 2

With all of the discussion of conspiracy theories in the news media … and advocacy for conspiracy theories on the Internet (Uscinski et al., 2016, p. 2)

05 — Counterclaims

Opposing Perspectives

Click each item to expand the counterclaim and supporting evidence.

Conspiracy theories do not have a large impact because many individuals do not believe them +

Some people argue that conspiracy theories don't always have a large impact because many people often don't believe them and end up rejecting them. This suggests that the concern over conspiracy theories may be unnecessary.

Evidence for Counterclaim

Many individuals without strong conspiratorial predispositions tend to reject conspiracy claims. (Uscinski et al. 471)

Conspiracy theories are caused primarily by psychological traits like paranoia, not social media +

Some researchers argue that belief in conspiracy theories is constructed in individual psychological characteristics like anxiety, paranoia, or a need for cognitive closure. Moving forward with this view, targeting social media platforms would do little to reduce conspiracy belief because the underlying cause is rather psychological.

Evidence for Counterclaim

A large body of research in psychology suggests that conspiracy beliefs are driven by anxiety, paranoia, and feelings of powerlessness (e.g., Grzesiak-Feldman 2013) (Uscinski et al. 67).

Platforms can eliminate conspiracy theories by removing harmful content +

Some believe that social media companies already have the tools and willingness to address misinformation effectively. Platforms like YouTube, Facebook, and Twitter have implemented content moderation policies, fact-checking labels, and removal of posts that violate community guidelines. According to this argument, moderation efforts are sufficient to contain the spread of conspiracy theories.

Evidence for Counterclaim

Platforms have attempted to moderate misinformation and remove harmful posts. (Thomas 13)

06 — Refutations

Responding to Opposition

Counterclaim 1

Many individuals without conspiratorial predispositions reject conspiracy claims, suggesting their societal impact is limited.

Refutation 1

Even if many individuals reject conspiracy theories, those who already don't trust institutions are more likely to believe and spread them. The harm lies in the targeted reach these theories have among vulnerable populations which is often enough to influence political and social issues.

Counterclaim 2

Conspiracy belief is primarily a product of psychological traits like paranoia, making social media interventions ineffective.

Refutation 2

The missing aspect in this paragraph is the role played by one's social and political environment in determining their beliefs. Research indicates that political cues and contextual factors greatly influence individuals' belief in conspiracy theories (Jolley et al. 472), therefore indicating that one's belief is not only an individual characteristic.

Counterclaim 3

Social media platforms can eliminate conspiracy theories through content moderation and post removal.

Refutation 3

With even moderation, the algorithm is set up to maximize engagement, which conspiracy theories are often good at achieving. "A small number of very active users were able to exploit the recommendations system to promote the ID2020 conspiracy theory" (Thomas 13). This illustrates that moderation cannot overcome the intrinsic motivations embedded within the system design.

08 — Social Media & Spread

How Conspiracy Theories Spread

Conspiracies have transformed with the advent of social media platforms. Conspiracies, which previously took months or even years to propagate, are now spread throughout the globe within a matter of hours. The algorithms on these social media platforms are biased towards content with higher interaction rates, and conspiracy theories are designed to generate emotional responses from the audience. This forms a vicious circle, whereby the more interactions, the higher visibility.

Psychological and social variables also have their significance. When people are exposed to information only in agreement with their opinions, the echo chamber effect occurs, thereby intensifying distrust and reducing acceptance to correction. Alongside anonymity and speed of dissemination of information on social media, these psychological phenomena are responsible for the growing trend of conspiracies today.

09 — Conclusion

Final Thoughts & Open Questions

Conclusion

Conspiracy theories are not made up haphazardly but thoughtfully constructed through fear, biases, and selective pieces of information. As indicated by this essay's three arguments, conspiracy theories are dependent on institutional distrust, blend facts with fake news to seem plausible, and flourish due to the nature of social media that favors interaction over verification. Even though some people may be skeptical of the theories, they continue posing risks to contemporary societies.

Solving this problem is not as simple as regulating these platforms. There must be an investment made into educating people on how to think critically and understand the difference between reality and fiction.

Future Research Questions
  • How do algorithmic recommendation systems differ in their amplification of conspiracy content across platforms like TikTok, YouTube, and Facebook?
  • What long-term effects does early exposure to conspiracy theories have on adolescents' critical thinking and civic engagement?
  • Can media literacy education meaningfully reduce susceptibility to conspiracy theories, and at what age is intervention most effective?
  • How should governments and platforms balance free speech protections with the need to limit dangerous misinformation?
